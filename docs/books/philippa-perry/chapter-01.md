# Chapter 1: Your Parenting Legacy

## Part One: The Parent You Were Given

Children do not do what we say; they do what we do. When it comes to raising our children, there is no escaping the fact that how we were parented ourselves influences the way we parent. It is one of the most significant things that shapes us as people and as parents.

Your early experiences lay down patterns in your brain — patterns of what to expect from relationships, from the world, and from yourself. These patterns can serve you well, or they can trip you up, especially when you become a parent and face situations that unconsciously remind you of your own childhood.

The good news is that you do not have to be a prisoner of your past. Understanding the patterns you inherited is the first step toward choosing which ones to keep and which ones to change. This is not about blaming your parents. It is about becoming conscious of the invisible forces that shape your reactions so that you can respond to your child rather than simply react.

## Rupture and Repair

Every parent makes mistakes. What matters is not perfection but what happens afterward — whether you can acknowledge the rupture and work to repair it. A rupture might be shouting when you did not mean to, dismissing your child's feelings, or being distracted when they needed your attention. These moments are inevitable.

Repair means going back to your child and acknowledging what happened. It means saying something like, "I was wrong to shout at you. I was feeling frustrated, and I took it out on you. I am sorry." This teaches your child something profound: that relationships can withstand conflict, that mistakes do not mean the end of love, and that people can take responsibility for their actions.

The capacity to repair is more important than the capacity to avoid rupture. In fact, it is through the cycle of rupture and repair that children learn emotional resilience. They learn that difficult feelings can be survived, that relationships are robust enough to handle disagreement, and that they themselves are worthy of an apology.

## Where Does This Emotion Come From?

When you feel a surge of anger or anxiety in response to something your child has done, it is worth pausing to ask yourself: where does this emotion come from? Is this reaction proportionate to what just happened? Or does it belong to something older — something from your own childhood that has been triggered?

A mother named Tay described how her daughter Emily got stuck at the top of a jungle gym. Tay's immediate reaction was a flash of anger — not at the danger, but at what she perceived as her daughter's helplessness. She wanted to shout, "Why did you climb up there if you cannot get down?"

When she reflected on this later, Tay realized that helplessness was something she had been punished for as a child. Her parents had no patience for it. So when she saw it in her daughter, it triggered the same fear and frustration she had felt as a little girl — the feeling that helplessness was dangerous and unacceptable.

A week later, Emily got stuck on the jungle gym again. This time, Tay took a breath. Instead of rescuing her daughter or scolding her, she stood nearby and said, "I can see you are stuck. What do you think you could try?" Emily figured it out on her own. The shift was not dramatic from the outside, but internally, Tay had broken a pattern that had been passed down through generations.

This is the work of conscious parenting: noticing when your emotional response belongs to the past rather than the present, and choosing to respond differently. It does not require perfection. It requires curiosity about yourself and compassion for the child you once were.
